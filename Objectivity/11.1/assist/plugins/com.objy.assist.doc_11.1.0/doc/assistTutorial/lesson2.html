Lesson 2: Objectivity/DB and Traditional DBMS
Objectivity/DB differs from traditional database-management systems (DBMSs) in terms of data format, data organization, files, and processes.
Data format
Objectivity/DB stores persistent objects as they are defined by your application; their format closely resembles their in-memory representation. Persistent objects can therefore have any number of attributes whose values are primitive data types, structures, references to other persistent objects, relationships to other persistent objects, or even variable-size arrays.
A traditional DBMS stores data as tables of records—fixed-length sequences of primitive values, with at most one variable-length field. A relational database management system (RDBMS) represents all data in rows and columns, equivalent to the records and fields in a traditional DBMS. With these approaches, objects produced by an object-oriented application cannot be stored as the application sees them—they must be transformed to the format required by the database.
Data organization
In Objectivity/DB, objects are connected to each other by references and relationships. Ancillary objects such as indexes, trees, and collections can be created to facilitate fast lookup.
In a traditional DBMS, data is organized around indexes (ISAM), trees (IMS), sets (CODASYL), or tables that support structured queries (RDBMS using SQL). An RDBMS can store objects, but imposes its organization on them. That is, object attributes and relationships must be broken into rigid rows and columns for storage in tables.
Process structure
A traditional DBMS localizes its database services in a server process that is distinct from client applications.
In Objectivity/DB, concurrency management is normally localized in a separate process (the lock server). Most other database services are provided as a library you can build into your applications. You get to structure your applications as client, server, or middleware to suit your requirements. You can run your applications on different computers in a network, although typically you want each application close to the database files it will be accessing.
